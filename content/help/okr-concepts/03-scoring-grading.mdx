# Scoring & Grading OKRs

OKRs are scored on a **0.0 to 1.0 scale** at the end of each cycle. Scoring is the mechanism that makes OKRs a learning tool, not just a goal-setting exercise.

## The Scale

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **1.0** | Fully achieved — and you should ask: "Was this ambitious enough?" |
| **0.7** | The sweet spot — ambitious goal, strong execution |
| **0.4–0.6** | Partial progress — worth understanding why |
| **0.0–0.3** | Missed — requires honest reflection |

<Callout type="tip">
If your team consistently scores 1.0, your Key Results aren't ambitious enough. Doerr's target is an average of **0.6–0.7**.
</Callout>

## Why 0.7 Is the Goal

OKRs are designed to be **stretch goals**. A team that scores 0.7 on an ambitious KR has accomplished more meaningful work than a team that scores 1.0 on a conservative KR.

> *"If you set a crazy, ambitious goal and miss it, you'll still achieve something remarkable."* — Larry Page

## How to Score a Key Result

Most KRs are scored by calculating progress on the underlying metric:

```
score = (actual_value - start_value) / (target_value - start_value)
```

**Example:** KR is "Increase NPS from 30 to 60"
- At cycle end, NPS is 51
- Score = (51 - 30) / (60 - 30) = 21/30 = **0.7**

## Scoring Is Not Performance Review

<Callout type="warning">
OKR scores must never be used directly in performance reviews or compensation decisions. If they are, people will set sandbagged (easy) goals to score 1.0.
</Callout>

Score conversations should focus on: *"What did we learn? What do we do differently next cycle?"* — not *"Who is to blame for the 0.4?"*

## Objective-Level Grade

After scoring all Key Results under an Objective, average the scores for an overall Objective grade. Use this to prioritize which Objectives to carry forward, extend, or drop next cycle.
