# CFRs: Conversations, Feedback, Recognition

CFRs are the continuous performance management practices that Doerr added to the OKR framework in *Measure What Matters*. They replace the annual performance review cycle with ongoing dialogue.

> *"OKRs are the what. CFRs are the how."* — John Doerr

## The Three Parts

### Conversations

Regular 1:1 check-ins between manager and contributor focused on:
- Progress toward OKRs
- Blockers and support needed
- Learning and growth

These replace the annual review with **ongoing dialogue**. Doerr recommends at minimum monthly; many high-performing teams do weekly.

### Feedback

Real-time, two-way input on work and behavior:
- **Peer feedback** — team members give input on collaboration
- **Upward feedback** — contributors give managers honest input
- **In-the-moment** — tied to specific events, not accumulated over a year

<Callout type="tip">
Good feedback is specific and behavioral: *"In the Monday standup, your status update helped me unblock my work immediately"* — not *"You're a good communicator."*
</Callout>

### Recognition

Acknowledging contributions publicly and in the moment:
- Tied to specific behaviors and outcomes
- Not just milestone-based (anniversary, promotion)
- Peer-to-peer, not only manager-to-report

<Callout type="note">
Recognition doesn't require budget. A public shout-out in team chat or all-hands carries significant weight when it's specific and timely.
</Callout>

## Why CFRs Matter for OKRs

OKRs without CFRs become a tracking exercise. CFRs are what make OKRs a **culture of accountability and learning** rather than a reporting burden.

| Without CFRs | With CFRs |
|-------------|-----------|
| OKR scores revealed at quarter-end surprise | Weekly check-ins surface problems early |
| Goals feel handed down from above | Conversations align goals to individual motivation |
| Poor performers hide behind team metrics | Feedback creates individual accountability |
| Recognition is rare and formal | Wins are celebrated in real time |
