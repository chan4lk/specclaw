# Common OKR Mistakes

OKRs are simple in concept and hard in practice. These are the patterns Doerr and practitioners consistently identify as failure modes.

## 1. Writing Tasks as Key Results

**The mistake:** KRs that describe activities, not outcomes.

> ❌ "Launch the new onboarding flow"
> ✅ "Reduce time-to-first-value from 14 days to 3 days"

Activities can be done without achieving anything. Outcomes force you to measure whether the activity worked.

---

## 2. Too Many OKRs

**The mistake:** Setting 8–12 Objectives to cover everything.

<Callout type="warning">
More than 5 Objectives per cycle destroys focus. If everything is a priority, nothing is.
</Callout>

OKRs are about **triage** — choosing what matters most and committing resources to it. Setting too many is avoiding the hard decision of what *not* to do.

---

## 3. Sandbagging (Setting Easy Goals)

**The mistake:** Setting KRs you know you'll hit at 1.0 to look successful.

This happens when OKR scores are tied to performance reviews or compensation. The fix: keep OKRs separate from eval cycles and celebrate 0.7 as success.

---

## 4. Set-and-Forget OKRs

**The mistake:** Drafting OKRs in January and revisiting them in December.

Without weekly check-ins, OKRs become irrelevant quickly. Teams solve problems that no longer exist or miss emerging priorities. The cadence (weekly CFRs, monthly reviews) is not optional.

---

## 5. No Vertical Alignment

**The mistake:** Team OKRs that don't connect to company Objectives.

If a team is optimizing for something the company doesn't care about this quarter, effort is wasted. Check that each team Objective traces to at least one company-level Objective.

---

## 6. Treating OKRs as a Reporting Tool

**The mistake:** OKR check-ins become status updates to management, not team learning moments.

The purpose of scoring is to **learn and adjust**, not to report upward. If your OKR review meetings feel like performance reviews, the culture is off.

<Callout type="tip">
Reframe the weekly OKR check-in question from "How much did you accomplish?" to "What did you learn, and what will you do differently?"
</Callout>

---

## 7. Ignoring the "Why" (No Mission Connection)

**The mistake:** OKRs that don't connect to the organization's mission or purpose.

Doerr emphasizes starting with *why* — the mission — before setting goals. Teams that understand the mission can craft OKRs that genuinely serve it, rather than proxies that game the system.
